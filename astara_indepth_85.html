01WHY NOW

A credible growth path

Since the full-scale Russia-Ukraine war, and especially since 2024, automotive and mobility players have started to pivot, either totally or through specific products and services, toward the defense industry.

This is not a local trend. It is a phenomenon that has spread across the world. We refer to the Russia-Ukraine war because it has acted as a tipping point in how industrialized countries perceive security, industry and military preparedness. Of course, wars have always existed. The difference is that many of them were far enough away, or disconnected enough from major powers, to avoid directly reshaping the countries and economies that define global industrial priorities.

We have entered an era of asymmetric warfare driven by information, autonomy and cost imbalance, where a $10,000 drone can neutralize a $500,000 missile.

This war, and now the Iran-Israel war, have shown the world a major evolution, perhaps even a revolution, in warfare. It is comparable to other major military transformations in history. We have entered an era of asymmetric warfare driven by information, autonomy and cost imbalance, where a $10,000 drone can neutralize a $500,000 missile.

This is why, since 2024, dual-use industrialization has moved from being a marginal strategy to becoming a credible growth path for automotive, mobility and transport companies. This shift is being pulled by global rearmament and defense-industrial policy, and pushed by commercial pressure inside the civilian mobility sector. Companies are not simply “choosing defense”. They are responding to a combination of new demand, available public money, strategic urgency and pressure in their original markets. Companies are doing what they always do: making the most of the context they face.

Automotive and mobility companies have the know-how, products and software capabilities that modern warfare increasingly needs. They have expertise in vehicles, batteries, sensors, autonomy, robotics, fleet management, manufacturing and software integration. For that reason, many of them are willing to offer these capabilities to the defense sector in order to increase revenues and diversify their business. In exchange, the defense sector is absorbing civilian mobility capabilities at increasing speed.

Defense has become a much more attractive end market because budgets and policy signals have changed.

02GLOBAL REARMAMENT

Eleven years of growth

The strongest figures to anchor this shift are global. The Stockholm International Peace Research Institute, SIPRI, an independent international organization dedicated to research on conflict, armaments, arms control and disarmament, estimates that world military expenditure reached $2.887 trillion in 2025, up 2.9% in real terms over 2024. This marked the 11th consecutive year of growth. Between 2016 and 2025, global military spending rose by 41%. Europe increased spending by 14% in 2025, while Asia and Oceania increased it by 8.1%.

NATO allies also committed at The Hague Summit to invest 5% of GDP annually in defense by 2035, with 3.5% of GDP dedicated to core defense requirements and 1.5% to broader defense-related investments. China announced a 7% increase in its 2026 defense budget, reaching around 1.91 trillion yuan, while Taiwan proposed 2026 defense spending of NT$949.5 billion, equivalent to 3.32% of GDP. Japan also approved a record FY2026 defense budget of about ¥9.04 trillion, or $58 billion, including investments in unmanned systems and long-range capabilities.

In this new warfare context, preparing the manufacturing base, supply chains and software stack needed to develop autonomous systems (mostly drones, but in many different shapes and formats) has become one of the central priorities. Governments know this, and they are shaping policy accordingly. One clear example is the US Replicator initiative, which explicitly focuses on scalable, all-domain, attritable autonomous systems and the software needed to enable them. Another example is Europe's Readiness 2030 / ReArm Europe agenda, which aims to mobilize up to €800 billion in additional defense spending by 2030. At the same time, EU member-state defense expenditure was estimated to reach €381 billion in 2025.

At the core of this hardware and software development are both incumbents and startups, including many automotive and mobility companies. In many ways, they have been preparing for this moment for the last two decades — not necessarily because they were thinking about defense, but because the technologies they developed for civilian mobility are now becoming strategically relevant for modern warfare.

03METHODOLOGY

How we have classified the cases

Type of pivot
  • New defense product line
    The company had no defense business before 2024 and is building a specific new product.
  • Existing defense arm scaling up
    The company already had a defense division before 2024 and is now growing it faster.
  • Factory/capacity repurposed
    A specific site is being converted to defense use, often for someone else's product.
  • Corporate divestiture
    The company is exiting automotive entirely to go pure-play defense (Rheinmetall).
  • Dual-use startup
    A mobility-tech startup whose core tech serves both civilian and military markets simultaneously.
Product category
  • Ground Vehicle
    Any wheeled or tracked vehicle.
  • Aerial Vehicle
    Anything that flies.
  • Missile / Munition Components
    Interceptors, launchers, missile system parts.
  • Autonomy / Software
    The product is the software.
  • Satellite & Space
    Space-based comms/systems.
  • Manufacturing Capacity
    The company doesn't have one specific defense product. It's converting a factory, leasing space to others, offering contract manufacturing, or restructuring the whole business toward defense generally.
